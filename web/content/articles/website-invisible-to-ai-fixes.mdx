A beautiful website can be completely invisible to AI. If the things a customer asks about (what you do, where, and roughly what it costs) are locked inside images, hidden behind "call for a quote," or buried in a page AI can't load, then as far as the AI is concerned that information doesn't exist. Here are seven fixes that make your site readable.

## Why can't AI read my website even though it looks fine?

Because AI reads text, not pictures. A site that looks great to a human can be empty to a machine if its key information is inside images, PDFs or design elements rather than real text. AI doesn't see your gorgeous hero banner; it reads the words it can extract. If the words aren't there as text, you've given it nothing to recommend you on, no matter how good the page looks.

## Fix 1: Get your services out of images and into plain text

AI can't read a service list inside a JPG, a logo or an infographic. Take every service that's currently shown only as a graphic and write it out as real text on the page: "Burst pipe repair. Blocked drains. Hot water systems. Gas fitting." Plain words an engine can lift straight into an answer. The picture can stay for looks, but the words have to exist as text underneath it.

## Fix 2: Replace "call for a quote" with real, plain information

"Call for a quote" and nothing else gives the AI zero to work with. You don't have to publish a full price list, but give the engine something concrete: what you do, where you do it, and a "starting from" or callout figure where you can. "Same-day blocked drains across Perth's northern suburbs, callout from $X." That one sentence is something AI can pick up and use. A page that only says "contact us" is a page the AI skips.

## Fix 3: Put your prices or "starting from" figures in writing

A price inside a downloadable PDF rate card is invisible; a price written on the page is readable. The 40-second version: AI rarely opens and parses your PDF flyer, but it easily reads a line of text. Even rough guidance ("most callouts $X–$Y," "fixed-price safety switch install from $X") gives the AI a concrete, quotable detail that makes it more confident recommending you over a competitor who hid everything behind a form.

## Fix 4: Spell out your service area, suburb by suburb

"We service Perth" is too vague for a suburb-level question. When someone asks AI for a sparky in Scarborough, the AI is matching against text. A page that lists "Scarborough, Trigg, Karrinyup, Doubleview and the surrounding northern beaches" is a direct match. A page that just says "all of Perth" is a weak one. Write out the real suburbs you cover as text on the page.

## Fix 5: Make sure the page actually loads, and fast

A slow, broken or JavaScript-only page can stop AI reading you at all. If your important text only appears after heavy scripts run, or the page takes ages to load, some crawlers and AI systems give up before they get your content. Page speed has been a Google ranking factor for years and the same fundamentals help AI: get your key information into the initial HTML as real text, keep the page quick, and don't bury everything behind interactions.

> The businesses that get skipped aren't doing bad work. They're the ones the AI couldn't read.

## Fix 6: Use clear headings and answer real questions

AI loves pages structured the way people ask questions. Use plain, question-style headings ("How much does a hot water system replacement cost?", "Do you do emergency callouts?", "Which suburbs do you cover?") and answer each one directly in the first sentence or two underneath. This is exactly how AI engines like to find and quote information, and it doubles as genuinely useful content for the humans who land on the page.

## Fix 7: Match your website to your profile and directories

If your website says one thing and your Google Business Profile or hipages listing says another, AI hesitates. In our test of 48 Perth searches across three AI engines, just over half of everything the AI cited was directories and reviews, not business websites, so the engine is cross-checking what your site says against what the rest of the web says. Same business name, same phone, same services, same suburbs everywhere. Consistency is what turns "this might be them" into a confident recommendation. (More on that in our [research on what AI actually picked](/articles/we-asked-chatgpt-best-tradies-perth/).)

## How do I know if my site is currently readable?

The quick test: imagine someone read your homepage aloud over the phone, with no screen. Would they know what you do, where, and roughly what it costs? If the answer is "no, you'd have to look at the pictures," then AI is in the same boat, and it's skipping you. The fix is always the same: turn the important stuff into plain, consistent, quick-loading text.
