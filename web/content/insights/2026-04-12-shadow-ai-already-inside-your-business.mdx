A small finance team in Perth ran an internal survey last quarter. Forty-one percent of staff admitted they had pasted client information into a public AI tool in the previous month. Eighteen percent admitted they did it weekly. Nobody had been told they could. Nobody had been told they could not.

This is shadow AI, and if you have not asked the question in your own business, the answer is the same.

## What it actually looks like

Shadow AI is rarely the dramatic story. It is the bookkeeper pasting a creditor reconciliation into ChatGPT to ask why the figures will not balance. It is the office manager dropping a draft warning letter into Claude to soften the tone. It is the apprentice estimator photographing a tender document on their phone and asking an app to summarise it during the lunch break.

None of those people think of themselves as data thieves. They are trying to do the job faster. The tool is on their phone. The boss has not said no. The competitor down the road is presumably doing the same thing.

The data, however, is now sitting in a third-party model provider's logs, possibly in another jurisdiction, with terms of service the staff member has not read.

## Why blanket bans do not work

The instinct of most owners, when they hear about this, is to ban it. Send an email. Update the handbook. Block the URLs at the firewall.

That tactic has a six-week half-life. The tools are too useful and too accessible. Staff move them to personal phones. The clever ones use the web version through a private browser. You have not stopped the leakage; you have only stopped seeing it.

> Banning shadow AI does not remove it. It removes your visibility of it.

The honest position is that the genie is out of the bottle. Your job as the owner is not to push it back in. It is to give the team a sanctioned, equally convenient alternative, and to write down what good looks like.

## What good looks like

A workable shadow-AI position has three parts.

**First, a usage policy that distinguishes data classes.** Public marketing copy can go into any tool. De-identified internal text can go into approved tools. Customer-identifying information, financial data, and contracts go into nothing without sign-off. One page. Plain English. Signed by every staff member.

**Second, a sanctioned tool that is at least as easy as the consumer one.** If the only approved option is harder to access than the consumer ChatGPT account, the policy will be ignored. The sanctioned tool needs to be on the same phones, with the same speed, and with logging the business controls.

**Third, a quiet conversation, not a witch hunt.** Most staff using shadow AI are not malicious. They will switch to the sanctioned tool the day it becomes available, provided you do not punish them for the prior usage. Make the amnesty explicit.

## What we see in audits

When OperateAI runs the data sensitivity stage of an audit, shadow AI almost always surfaces. We do not ask in a way that demands confession. We ask which tools the team finds useful for which tasks, and we listen for the consumer-grade names. ChatGPT. Claude. Gemini. Notion AI. Otter.

The number that matters is not how many staff are using these tools. It is what data classes they are pasting in. A bookkeeper using ChatGPT to reformat a public contractor list is fine. A bookkeeper using ChatGPT to reformat a creditor ageing report with names attached is a notifiable data incident waiting to be noticed.

## The owner's call

If you are an owner reading this, two questions to put to your team this week:

1. What are you currently using AI for in your work?
2. What information is going into those tools?

Listen without judgment. Write down what you hear. That is your real starting position. Anything else is a fiction.

The follow-up is then straightforward: a written policy, a sanctioned tool, and a tidy line drawn under the prior period. That is the difference between a business that is exposed and a business that is in control.
